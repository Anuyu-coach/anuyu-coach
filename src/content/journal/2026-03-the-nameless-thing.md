<div class="post-epigraph">
  <blockquote>Whereof one cannot speak, thereof one must be silent.</blockquote>
  <cite>Ludwig Wittgenstein · Tractatus Logico-Philosophicus</cite>
</div>

I am a woman of words, and I say this not as a boast but as a confession: that
words have been my sanctuary, my scalpel, the one tool I have always reached
for when the world stopped making sense. Which, if I am honest, has been more
often than I would have liked. So it means quite a lot when I tell you that the
most real thing I know has no name. That I, who can dissect an emotion the way
a surgeon dissects tissue, who can find the precise word for almost any shade
of human experience, have arrived at something I cannot name, cannot locate,
cannot hand to you in a sentence and say: here, this is it.

I have tried many words. God. Spirit. The Universe. Higher Intelligence. Each
one felt almost right and then slightly wrong, like a shoe that fits but
pinches somewhere you can't quite locate.

## The conversation that started it all

A few years ago I was talking to an atheist friend. It was one of those
conversations, you know the kind, that begins somewhere completely ordinary
and ends somewhere you didn't plan for and couldn't have predicted. We
arrived, as these conversations tend to, at God, at prayer. At what people
actually mean when they reach for those words, because I have always been
more interested in what people mean than what they say.

He said he didn't believe in anything external. That when life got difficult,
he turned completely inward, relied on himself, trusted himself. And always,
eventually, came through.

I couldn't help but ask the question underneath the question:
*"When you turn inward like that, do you believe you will find the answer?"*

And he said, without hesitation: *"Yes. Always."*

I found the missing piece right there, in that single word. *Always.*

He wasn't relying on nothing. He was relying on the same infinite intelligence
that the believer calls God, the mystic calls Spirit, the scientist calls the
subconscious mind, and the philosopher (Wittgenstein included) eventually
runs out of language to describe. Different words, the same threshold. The
same turning inward toward something that, it seems, has always been there,
waiting with considerably more patience than we give it credit for.

## What prayer used to mean

I grew up more spiritual than religious, though I understand both from the
inside, having seen the beauty that lives in religious tradition and, I will
not pretend otherwise, the blood that has been spilled in its name. I have
sat with people from every background, every faith, every kind of disbelief,
and the one thing I have noticed, the thing that strikes me every single
time, is this: when life gets hard enough, everyone turns somewhere. No one,
not one person, simply stands in the open with nowhere to go.

For most of my earlier life, my prayer was a reaching outward, a solemn
request sent to something larger than me. Asking to be heard, asking to be
helped, asking for the strength and patience to make it through. And
sometimes, when the strength wasn't there, simply surrendering what I could
not carry alone, giving it to something higher, genuinely hoping, that it
would be received.

That version of prayer held me. I am not dismissing it. It would be
dishonest and ungrateful to dismiss it, because there were nights when it
was the only thing holding me upright.

But I understand it very differently now.

## When the storm finally settled

I distinctly remember a period in my life marked by a noise so loud: the
self-criticism, the shame, the spinning wheel of blame that just kept
turning endlessly. And that nothing could silence it. Not prayer in the
old sense. Not meditation. Not any teaching or guided practice or self-help
framework I had ever encountered. Nothing could reach the place that needed
reaching.

The nameless thing itself felt unreachable, buried under it. As though
whatever intelligence had always quietly accompanied me had simply gone
underwater and I could no longer find it from the surface.

Until I surrendered. Not gracefully, and I want to be clear about that.
Because the word *surrender* has been made to sound like something
peaceful and intentional, and this was neither. This was the surrender of
someone who had run out of every other option, who had tried everything,
who finally lay down not as a spiritual practice but as the only thing
left.

And in that particular quality of silence, something became visible that
had been there the entire time, quietly, without needing my acknowledgment
to exist.

The nameless thing was not outside me. It had never been outside me. It
was not a master sitting somewhere above, granting wishes from a distance.
It was not a force I needed to petition or appease or stretch myself
toward. It was me. It had always, always been me.

## The realisation that rearranged everything

For a time longer than I care to admit, I carried the quiet, persistent
ache of feeling alone. Of surviving without anyone coming to save me. Of
looking around in my darkest moments and finding no one there, and taking
that absence personally, as though it meant something about my worth or
my luck or what I deserved.

And then, in the stillness, the thought shifted. It wasn't dramatic or
loud like a lightning bolt. More like a slow turning of light, the way
dawn arrives and suddenly I could see:

> No one came to save me. But I have been saving myself my whole life.
> I was never alone. I was with me. The nameless thing is me. And so am I.
> I am me.

Wittgenstein was right: some things cannot be spoken, only arrived at,
only felt in the body, in the bones. In the particular silence after a
storm that you were certain would never end. The moment you stop trying
to name the thing and simply let yourself be held by it, you realise,
with something between shock and deep recognition, that you were holding
yourself all along.

## What prayer means to me now

My prayer now is not a request. It is not addressed outward. It does
not go looking for something beyond me. It stopped knocking on a door
outside myself and waiting to be let in.

It is an internal dialogue, between me and the deepest, most honest,
least performed version of myself. The intelligence that was always
there beneath the noise, beneath the spinning wheel, beneath the years
of running. The part that kept me moving and reaching toward something
true. Even in the long stretches when I could not have told you what
that something was, or whether it existed at all.

There are still moments. A particular quality of light at sunset, the
impossible, breathtaking intricacy of human anatomy, the silence of
deep space which makes me feel simultaneously very small and very
protected. When I pause and feel the distinct possibility of something
that exceeds all of this, something vast and quietly intelligent, and I
do not close the door on that possibility.

I hold it with curiosity, not certainty. I don't need to name it to
trust it. I have learned, finally, that the unnamed can still be real.

## We are all pointing at the same thing

The believer and the atheist. The mystic and the scientist. The person
on their knees in a temple and the person who sits in silence and
simply knows, with that particular unshakeable knowing that has nothing
to do with proof, that they will find the answer.

Different words. Different rituals. Different names for the nameless
thing.

But the same intelligence. The same turning inward. The same infinite
resource that was never, not once, not for a single moment, outside of
you.

Next time something in you bristles at another person's language for
this: their God, their Universe, their Self, their *Always*.
Just notice. Underneath the different words, we are all knocking on
the same door. And the door, it turns out, was never locked. We just
kept looking for the handle on the wrong side.

I will leave you with one question. Sit with it whenever you are
finally still enough to hear yourself think:

> If the thing you have been searching for outside yourself was already
> within you, what would change about how you live your life?
